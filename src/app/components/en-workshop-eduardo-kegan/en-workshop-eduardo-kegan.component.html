Procrastination is a very common phenomenon. Everybody procrastinates, but not everyone is a chronic procrastinator. However, chronic procrastination is a common problem, affecting about 1 in 5 people. Procrastination is particularly common in academia or work. It is not a disorder, but a maladaptive behavior that may serve different functions in different contexts. Varieties of procrastination and different theories to account for them will be presented. We shall analyze the relationship between procrastination and other clinical phenomena, such as avoidance, perfectionism, impulsivity and sensation-seeking.

Although procrastination can be clearly broken down into other behaviors that have been subject to profound scrutiny in CBT, it is nevertheless such a common problem that is worthy of clinical attention. This workshop will help clinicians conduct a functional analysis of procrastination and, based on this, devise a therapeutic plan to overcome this typically self-defeating behavior.
